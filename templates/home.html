Diabetes

Diabetes is a chronic metabolic disorder characterized by high blood sugar levels, which occur when the body either does not produce enough insulin or cannot effectively use the insulin it produces. Insulin is a hormone made by the pancreas that allows glucose (sugar) to enter the cells of the body and be used for energy. Without proper insulin function, glucose builds up in the bloodstream, leading to various complications over time. Insulin is a hormone that regulates blood glucose. Hyperglycaemia, also called raised blood glucose or raised blood sugar, is a common effect of uncontrolled diabetes and over time leads to serious damage to many of the body's systems, especially the nerves and blood vessels.

Type 1 Diabetes (T1D)

Type 1 diabetes typically develops in children and young adults, but it can appear at any age.

Symptoms (T1D)

  • Increased thirst and urination
  • Extreme hunger
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Fatigue
  • Blurred vision
  • Irritability

Treatment (T1D)

  • Daily insulin injections
  • Blood sugar monitoring
  • Dietary management
  • Regular physical activity

Type 2 Diabetes (T2D)

Type 2 diabetes often develops in adults over the age of 45, but it is increasingly being diagnosed in younger individuals, including children, due to rising obesity rates and sedentary lifestyles.

Symptoms (T2D)

  • Increased thirst and frequent urination
  • Fatigue
  • Blurred vision
  • Slow-healing sores
  • Frequent infections

Treatment (T2D)

  • Lifestyle changes
  • Oral medications like Metformin
  • Insulin therapy for severe cases

Gestational Diabetes

Gestational diabetes is diagnosed during the second or third trimester of pregnancy.

Symptoms (Gestational)

  • Often no symptoms

Treatment (Gestational)

  • Blood sugar monitoring
  • Dietary management
  • Regular physical activity
  • Insulin therapy if needed
